Essential Seismic Safety Steps Before an Earthquake

Taking proactive steps before an earthquake strikes can significantly reduce injury and property damage while helping your family recover faster. Key preparedness actions include creating a simple emergency plan, securing your home against seismic hazards, and assembling a ready-to-go earthquake kit. These measures provide both practical protection and psychological confidence when an event occurs.


Creating an effective family earthquake plan starts with involving everyone in the household. Walk through the plan together so each person understands their role and knows exactly what to do when shaking begins. Identify at least two meeting points—one near home and one outside the neighborhood—in case your primary location becomes inaccessible. Agree on communication methods such as text messages, social apps, or a designated out-of-town contact to check in with if family members become separated.


Home preparation involves securing heavy furniture, appliances, and utilities. Anchor bookcases, cabinets, and tall furniture to wall studs using brackets, straps, or earthquake anchors. Learn where and how to shut off gas, water, and electricity to prevent leaks and fires after severe shaking. Use appliance safety straps to fasten water heaters, refrigerators, and other large items that could tip or slide during a quake. These steps address secondary hazards that often cause significant damage after the initial event.


Research confirms that household preparedness levels remain generally low, with perceived benefits, cues to action, and self-efficacy serving as significant predictors of whether families actually prepare. Understanding these factors helps explain why practice and tangible planning steps matter—families who see clear benefits, receive prompts to act, and believe in their ability to prepare are most likely to follow through with meaningful preparedness measures.

What to Do During an Earthquake to Stay Safe

How you react during an earthquake matters as much as your preparation. The most reliable protective action both indoors and outdoors is the "Drop, Cover, and Hold On" method. This technique involves dropping to your hands and knees so you cannot be knocked over, moving under a sturdy table or desk to cover your head and neck, and holding on to your shelter while being ready to move with it until shaking stops.


If no sturdy furniture is available, position yourself in an inside corner away from windows and cover your head with your arms. This approach minimizes injury from falling or flying objects, which cause most earthquake-related injuries. The technique creates a small protected space around your body during the most dangerous moments of an event.


Safety protocols differ when you are in a vehicle or outdoors. If driving when shaking begins, pull over safely away from overpasses, bridges, power lines, and large trees, then remain in the vehicle until shaking stops. In open areas, move to a clear spot away from buildings, trees, and utility wires. Stay clear of any structures that might collapse and remain alert for falling debris throughout the event.


Understanding survival priorities helps frame your response. The rule of threes in survival emphasizes that immediate physical safety takes precedence—you can survive three minutes without air, three hours without shelter in harsh conditions, three days without water, and three weeks without food. During an earthquake, protecting yourself from immediate physical harm is the absolute priority.

How to Respond After an Earthquake

After shaking stops, move with intention rather than panic. Begin by checking yourself and those nearby for injuries. Use your first-aid kit to treat minor cuts, scrapes, and bruises—clean wounds thoroughly, apply appropriate dressings, and monitor for signs of infection. A well-stocked first-aid kit and basic medical training make a significant difference during the critical first hours after an earthquake.


Know when professional medical help is necessary. Seek immediate medical care for heavy bleeding, suspected broken bones, head injuries, difficulty breathing, or loss of consciousness. For injured individuals, keep them warm and still, provide reassurance, and avoid moving anyone with suspected spinal injury unless there is immediate danger requiring evacuation.


Assess your environment for structural damage before deciding whether to stay or evacuate. Look for new cracks in walls and foundations, sagging roofs, and doors or windows that no longer fit their frames properly. If damage appears severe or you smell gas, leave immediately and proceed to your predetermined meeting point. Grab your go bag on the way out if it is safe to do so.


Follow official guidance before returning to your home. Listen for instructions via emergency radio or apps, and when in doubt, err on the side of caution by waiting for professional structural inspection. Having both an evacuation plan and a shelter-in-place plan gives you flexibility to respond appropriately to whatever conditions you encounter after the shaking stops.

Building Your Earthquake Emergency Kit

A complete earthquake kit combines water, food, first aid, and essential tools that enable you to survive 72 hours or more without outside assistance. Pack these essentials in sturdy, portable containers stored in accessible locations so they are ready when needed. The goal is self-sufficiency during the critical period when emergency services may be overwhelmed or infrastructure compromised.


Water storage forms the foundation of your kit. Store at least one gallon per person per day for three days minimum—this covers drinking and basic hygiene needs. Supplement stored water with portable filtration options such as filter bottles, squeeze systems, or purification tablets that allow you to safely use questionable water sources. Combining storage with reliable treatment methods creates essential redundancy for this critical resource.


Emergency food supplies should prioritize calorie-dense, shelf-stable items that require minimal preparation. Energy bars, canned goods, and freeze-dried meals offer reliable nutrition when cooking facilities are unavailable. Check expiration dates regularly and replace items to keep your kit current. Some survival staples like properly stored rice, beans, and honey can last for decades, making them excellent long-term investments.


First-aid components round out your kit essentials. Stock adhesive bandages, sterile gauze, antiseptic wipes, medical tape, scissors, and any personal prescription medications. Do not overlook the importance of maintaining adequate supplies of prescription medications—this is often the most critical and most frequently forgotten element of emergency medical preparedness.

Communication Plans and Emergency Technology

Clear communication and agreed meeting locations reduce panic and speed family reunification after an earthquake. A simple plan that everyone practices occasionally keeps all household members aligned when disaster strikes. Include immediate family contacts, close neighbors who can assist, and a reliable out-of-town contact who can relay messages when local networks are congested.


Prioritize text messages and social media for non-urgent status updates, as these use less bandwidth than voice calls during network congestion. Designate one family member to make voice calls only in true emergencies. For situations where cellular networks fail completely, two-way radios provide reliable backup communication. Understanding the differences between FRS, GMRS, CB, and ham radio options helps you select appropriate equipment for your needs.


Include pets and important documents in your communication and evacuation planning. Pack pet supplies including food, water, medications, carriers, and comfort items. Store copies of identification documents, insurance policies, and medical records in waterproof containers or encrypted cloud backup. Knowing exactly where pets and paperwork are located reduces stress and speeds decision-making during evacuations.


Technology extends your capabilities during infrastructure disruptions. Portable power banks keep phones and radios functioning during outages, while solar chargers provide sustainable power for extended grid-down situations. Battery-operated lanterns with spare batteries ensure reliable lighting regardless of conditions. Layering power solutions—solar, battery, and manual—maintains essential functionality when you need it most. GPS devices and offline maps support navigation when roads are closed or signage damaged.
