Heritage surveys are essential tools in the field of heritage conservation and cultural preservation, particularly when it comes to Aboriginal cultural heritage. In Western Australia, these surveys ensure that development projects comply with legislative requirements and respect the cultural values of Aboriginal communities. Different types of heritage surveys cater to various needs and stakeholders, including Aboriginal corporations, Native Title bodies, developers, and mining or exploration entities. The Work Program Clearance Survey is designed for targeted, specific areas like access tracks or drill holes. It focuses on identifying and protecting cultural heritage values, often recording cultural information to a Site Avoidance level. This allows developers to avoid culturally significant areas efficiently. Real-time decision-making during these surveys is facilitated by the presence of company representatives, ensuring alternative routes or work areas can be swiftly considered if cultural sites are identified. The Work Area Clearance Survey, in contrast, is suited for broader areas where the exact locations of works are undetermined. While this approach provides flexibility, it can be more time-consuming and costly. However, it helps avoid the need for Ministerial consent under Section 18 of the Aboriginal Heritage Act (1972). Site Avoidance Surveys are conducted when tangible and intangible heritage is discovered. These surveys record the location and description of a site to enable proponents to avoid and protect any Aboriginal heritage identified. However, they don't provide a comprehensive description of the site's cultural values, which is necessary for the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Committee (ACHC) to make informed decisions under section 5 of the AHA (1972). Site Identification Surveys are crucial for applications seeking to disturb an Aboriginal site. These surveys thoroughly document the places affected by proposed developments, ensuring the ACHC can make informed determinations. They involve meaningful engagement with Aboriginal communities and assess a site's significance using frameworks like the Australia ICOMOS Burra Charter. While there is no single method for evaluating the significance of heritage sites, respecting the wishes of Traditional Custodians is fundamental, particularly when sensitive cultural information is involved. Throughout these processes, collaboration between proponents, Heritage Consultants, and Aboriginal communities is vital. This ensures that Traditional Custodians can express their views freely and participate fully in decision-making, capturing diverse perspectives and ensuring culturally appropriate assessments. By understanding and respecting the cultural significance of heritage sites, these surveys safeguard Aboriginal heritage while facilitating responsible development, paving the way for a harmonious coexistence between modern development and traditional values.
