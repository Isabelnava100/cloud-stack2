Why regulated and considered-purchase sectors need different SEO

Law firms, healthcare clinics, financial services and B2B professional services share something most generic SEO advice ignores: their buyers are cautious, regulated content has compliance friction, and trust signals matter more than volume. Generic "SEO best practices" assume e-commerce buying patterns — fast decisions, cart funnels, conversion sprints. That's not how someone chooses a solicitor for a divorce, a private clinic for a procedure, or a wealth manager for a seven-figure portfolio. Sector-specific SEO accounts for longer research cycles, regulatory disclosures, professional body listings, and the entity-level trust signals that AI search engines and Google E-E-A-T algorithms increasingly reward.

How AI search engines decide who gets cited

Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT search, Perplexity and Gemini all answer questions by synthesising content from multiple sources, then citing the ones they trust most. The signals they reward are not the same as classic Google ranking factors. Entity coherence matters — does your business present consistently across your site, schema markup, social profiles, Google Business Profile, professional body listings and trade press mentions? Content depth matters — do you publish substantive long-form pieces that answer the actual question a user is asking, not surface-level overviews? Authority context matters — are you cited by, or do you cite, sources the AI itself trusts? Optimising for AI citation is not a separate discipline; it is what good SEO looks like when the user-facing surface stops being a list of ten blue links and starts being a synthesised answer.

Choosing the right SEO partner: the questions to actually ask

The questions that filter signal from noise are not technical. "How many clients do you have, and how many am I one of?" tells you whether you will get senior attention or a junior delivery account. "Who, specifically, will do the work?" tells you whether the person you pitch to is the person you brief. "Show me an example of work you did last month that you would not have produced for a different client" tells you whether the engagement is thinking or templating. "What would you stop doing if I asked you to?" tells you whether the agency has a service catalogue or a strategy. "How do you measure success in sectors where leads are slow and high-value?" tells you whether they understand considered-purchase markets. Five questions, asked of any prospective SEO partner, will eliminate most of the wrong ones.

What "good SEO" looks like by sector

Sector-fit changes what good SEO actually means in practice. For a UK law firm, it usually means strengthening LegalService schema, cleaning up Google Business Profile across practice areas, getting cited in AI assistants for high-intent queries like "best [practice area] solicitor in [city]", and earning coverage in legal trade press for authority signals. For a private healthcare clinic, it means MedicalBusiness schema, treatment-specific landing pages that satisfy E-E-A-T expectations, review velocity strategy, local pack visibility, and AI search optimisation for "is [treatment] worth it" research queries. For an independent estate agent, it means competing against Rightmove and Zoopla on hyperlocal expertise, technical SEO for property listings inventory, area-guide content that the aggregators cannot generate, and local citations that anchor the brand to specific patches. For a financial services firm, it means FinancialService schema with regulatory transparency, entity strength across professional body listings, AI search visibility for considered-purchase research, and digital PR in financial trade press. For a B2B professional services business, it means content strategy mapped to long sales cycles, entity-level authority signals, AI search optimisation for vendor shortlists, and account-based content for named target accounts. Same consultant, same methodology, different application. That's what sector-specific SEO actually means.

Additional FAQ

What does a typical engagement look like?

Most engagements start with a paid audit — technical SEO, content, AI search visibility, entity strength, competitor positioning. From there, either a defined project with a clear deliverable or an ongoing retainer where I act as the senior SEO function inside your business.

Can you guarantee rankings?

No serious SEO consultant can guarantee specific rankings, and anyone who does should be avoided. What I can guarantee is senior, hands-on work, transparent reporting, and that the same person you brief is the person doing the work. Results follow from that.

What does an SEO audit cover?

Technical SEO (crawl, index, Core Web Vitals, schema, internal linking), on-page content against commercial intent, AI search visibility (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews citations and mentions), entity strength across the web, and competitor positioning. Delivered as a prioritised action list with quick wins and strategic investments split out.

Do you offer one-off projects as well as retainers?

Yes. Common one-off projects: a paid SEO audit, a migration plan and oversight, a technical clean-up, a schema implementation, an AI visibility deep-dive. Retainers make sense when there's ongoing work — content strategy, monthly reporting, optimisation cycles, sector watch.

Can you fix a Google penalty or manual action?

Yes, with the caveat that genuine manual actions require honest acknowledgement of what triggered them. Most "penalties" people worry about are algorithmic ranking drops, not actual manual actions — and the diagnosis matters more than the fix. Diagnostic work is part of the audit.

Why is the consultancy capped at four clients?

Because at five or more, work stops being senior and starts being supervised. Four clients lets me know each business properly, do the work myself, and respond quickly when something changes. It's not a marketing line — it's the only model that delivers the kind of work clients actually pay a premium for.

Do you work with other agencies or in-house teams?

Yes — about a third of engagements are alongside an existing in-house marketing function or another agency. I act as the senior SEO specialist they don't have, supplementing rather than replacing. No turf wars, no rebuilding from scratch. Clear scope on who owns what and where I add value.

What tools do you use?

Ahrefs and Semrush for keyword and competitor research, Screaming Frog for technical crawls, Google Search Console and Looker Studio for performance reporting, Rank Math for on-page SEO, Search Atlas for AI visibility tracking, and a small set of bespoke spreadsheets for entity and citation tracking. Tools support the work, they don't replace the thinking.

Do you do digital PR and link building?

Yes — digital PR for earned coverage in trade press and AI-cited sources is a core service, especially for regulated sectors where authority signals matter. I don't do paid links, PBNs, or guest post networks. Legitimate digital PR only.

What about local SEO and Google Business Profile?

Local SEO is one of the ten core services. That covers Google Business Profile optimisation, citation cleanup, review strategy, local schema markup, and geo-targeted content. Particularly relevant for law firms, healthcare clinics, estate agents and any business with a physical location or service area.
