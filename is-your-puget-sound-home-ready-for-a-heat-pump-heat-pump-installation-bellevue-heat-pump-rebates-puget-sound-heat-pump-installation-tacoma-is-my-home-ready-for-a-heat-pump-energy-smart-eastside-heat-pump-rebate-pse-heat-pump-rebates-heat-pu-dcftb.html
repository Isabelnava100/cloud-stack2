Is Your Puget Sound Home Ready for a Heat Pump? HVAC, Electrical & Rebate Checklist

Review Your Current Heating System

Your current heating system is one of the biggest factors in choosing the right heat pump. Start by identifying what you have now: Gas furnace Electric furnace Electric baseboard heat Wall heaters Oil heat Propane heat Existing heat pump Ductless mini-split Boiler or radiant system This matters because some rebates are tied to the type of system being replaced. For example, Puget Sound Energy’s heating rebates include incentives for qualified customers upgrading to energy-efficient heating systems. PSE currently lists a $1,500 rebate for replacing electric resistance heat with a qualifying ducted or ductless air-source heat pump, with additional income-based Efficiency Boost rebate opportunities. Homeowner Tip If your current system is over 12–15 years old, needs frequent repairs, creates uneven temperatures, or does not provide cooling, it may be worth comparing repair costs against a heat pump upgrade.

Assessing Electrical Needs for Heat Pump Installation

A heat pump is an electrical appliance. That means your home’s electrical panel must have enough capacity to support the new system safely. You may need an electrical review if your home has: An older 100-amp panel Limited breaker space Frequent breaker trips Plans for an EV charger Plans for a heat pump water heater Existing electric appliances An older Seattle, Tacoma, or Eastside home with outdated wiring Multiple electrification projects are happening at once This is where Home Comfort Alliance has a strong competitive advantage. Many HVAC companies can install the heat pump, but not every contractor can also evaluate and complete the electrical work needed to support the system. Home Comfort Alliance offers electrical services in the Seattle–Tacoma area, including wiring, panel upgrades, outlet and switch installation, lighting, generator hookups, EV charger installation, safety inspections, and emergency electrical repair. For heat pump planning, homeowners can also reference HCA’s electrical panel upgrade services. Homeowner Tip Do not assume you need a panel upgrade. Some homes have enough capacity already. Others may need a panel upgrade, subpanel, load calculation, or circuit work. A professional assessment should confirm what is actually required.

Navigating Rebates and Financing Options

Heat pump rebates vary by utility, city, income eligibility, existing heating source, equipment type, and contractor requirements. In the Puget Sound region, homeowners may be served by different utilities, including Puget Sound Energy, Seattle City Light, Tacoma Power, Snohomish PUD, Peninsula Light, and others. Puget Sound Energy Heat Pump Rebates Puget Sound Energy heating rebates are available for qualified residential customers upgrading to energy-efficient heating systems. PSE currently lists a $1,500 rebate for replacing electric resistance heating with a qualifying ducted or ductless air-source heat pump, plus an income-based Efficiency Boost rebate opportunity. Homeowners can also explore HCA’s heat pump installation support here: Heat pump installation from Home Comfort Alliance Energy Smart Eastside Heat Pump Rebates Energy Smart Eastside offers special heat pump incentives for residents of Bellevue, Issaquah, Kirkland, Mercer Island, Redmond, and Sammamish. The program states that its rebates can be combined with other utility incentives for thousands of dollars in potential savings. The Energy Smart Eastside heat pump rebate page currently lists a $1,000 heat pump rebate for homeowners who purchase a Mitsubishi, Bryant, or Carrier heat pump through a contractor in its trusted installer network. Seattle City Light and Seattle Clean Heat Program Seattle City Light’s Home Energy Solutions page states that Seattle households heating with oil can receive a $2,000 instant rebate when converting to a qualified heat pump system through a participating contractor. The Seattle Clean Heat Program also states that the City is offering a $4,000 bonus rebate on top of the $2,000 rebate for moderate-income households earning between 81% and 150% of area median income, with bonus rebates available for installations completed by September 30, 2026. Tacoma Power Heat Pump Rebates Tacoma Power’s residential heat pump rebate page currently lists rebates by existing heat source and system type. For homes with existing zonal or electric forced air heat, listed rebates include $1,000 for ductless heat pumps up to four units, $1,000 for air-source heat pumps, and $2,000 for variable-speed heat pumps. Important Rebate Note Rebate programs change often. Always confirm current availability, eligibility, equipment requirements, installation deadlines, income requirements, and contractor participation before making a final decision. Homeowners can start by contacting HCA through the Home Comfort Alliance contact page.

Evaluate Your Ductwork, Airflow and Home Layout

If your home already has ductwork, a ducted heat pump may be a good fit. But the ducts need to be checked before installation. Poor ductwork can reduce comfort and efficiency even if the heat pump itself is high quality. Your ductwork should be reviewed for: Leaks Poor insulation Undersized ducts Crushed or disconnected sections Weak airflow to certain rooms Return air limitations Dust buildup Hot and cold spots If your home does not have ducts, a ductless mini-split system may be a better option. Ductless systems are often used in homes with electric baseboard heat, additions, bonus rooms, finished basements, garages, and older homes where adding ductwork would be difficult. If dust, debris, pet dander, remodel residue, or airflow restrictions are part of the issue, Home Comfort Alliance also offers professional air duct cleaning in Seattle–Tacoma. HCA states that professional duct cleaning can help restore system efficiency, improve the air homeowners breathe, and extend the life of heating and cooling equipment. Homeowner Tip If one room is always colder or warmer than the rest of the house, mention that during your estimate. Comfort problems are often connected to airflow, duct design, insulation, or equipment sizing.

Clarify Your Cooling Needs

Many Puget Sound homeowners are not just replacing heat. They are also adding air conditioning for the first time. This is one of the biggest reasons heat pumps are attractive in Western Washington. A heat pump can provide both heating and cooling from one system. Think about: Do you currently have AC? Which rooms get too hot in summer? Do upstairs bedrooms stay warm at night? Does your home have large west-facing windows? Are you trying to cool the whole home or specific rooms? Do you want central cooling or zoned comfort? If your home has never had air conditioning, a heat pump may be a better long-term investment than installing central AC only, especially if your heating system is also aging. Homeowners comparing options can learn more through Home Comfort Alliance’s air conditioning services and heat pump installation services.

Look at Insulation, Windows, and Air Sealing

A heat pump works best when the home can hold conditioned air. Before upgrading, consider whether your home has: Poor attic insulation Drafty windows Air leaks around doors Uninsulated crawl spaces Poorly sealed ducts Rooms that never feel comfortable High energy bills despite normal usage This does not mean you must complete every home efficiency upgrade before installing a heat pump. But it does mean your contractor should help you understand how the condition of your home affects system sizing, comfort, and performance. For broader energy-efficiency guidance, homeowners can also review ENERGY STAR’s heat pump resources. Homeowner Tip If your home is drafty, a bigger system is not always the answer. Better airflow, proper sizing, duct improvements, and air sealing may do more for comfort than oversizing the equipment.

Consider Indoor Air Quality

Heat pump planning is also a good time to look at indoor air quality. In Western Washington, homeowners often deal with: Seasonal allergens Wildfire smoke events Pet dander Damp-weather odors Dusty ductwork Remodel dust Poor airflow Stale indoor air during colder months A heat pump upgrade may be a good time to consider: Better filtration Air purifiers Duct cleaning Ventilation improvements Humidity control Smart thermostat upgrades Whole-home comfort monitoring This is another place HCA can outperform single-service competitors. The company can connect HVAC, duct cleaning, and indoor air quality into one practical comfort plan instead of treating them as separate services.
